Modernizing an Old Flip Clock

Using Arduino, stepper motors, and modern electronics

This is my project where I retrofit an old flip clock with modern electronics, including an Arduino and a NEMA 17 stepper.

Hardware

The clock used in this project is an old Soundesign 3464 flip clock. To modernize it, I used an Arduino R4 WiFi as the main microcontroller, a NEMA 17 stepper motor to drive the flipping mechanism, and an L298N H-Bridge motor controller to drive the NEMA 17.

  • Old Flip Clock (Soundesign 3464)
  • Arduino R4 WiFi - arduino.cc
  • NEMA 17 Stepper Motor - amazon.com
  • L298N H-Bridge Motor Controller - amazon.com

Build Process

    Was it worth it to upgrade my clock?

    Absolutely! The clock radio I used was not in working condition. The clock didn't work, nor did the radio. For me this clock was the perfect candidate for a conversion. I did have to salvage 3 pieces from the original clock gearbox, the drive pinion and 2 sleeves for the drive pinion.

    Installing the stepper motor

    Installing the stepper motor required a little bit of experimentation. It was difficult to mount the motor using the original motor mount, but it was the sturdiest place to mount the motor. I designed and 3d printed a bracket that the motor could mount to using the existing motor mount standoffs. I also 3d printed two sleeves that would act as spacers for the motor mount. I printed a length of sleeve, then cut the sleeve to the appropriate length based on fit. By no means was this the perfect or best solution, but it was the best design for not totally disassembling and risk breaking the original clock mechanism.

    Wiring the Arduino, Motor Driver and LED's.

    Wiring the Arduino and Motor Driver was relatively straightforward. I first needed to identify the coils in the stepper, I then wired the motor controller accordingly. The IN1-IN4 on the l298n are wired to Arduino pins 8-11. 12V and GND on the l298n are wired to Arduino pins 5V and GND. OUT1 and OUT2 on the l298n are wired to the motor. Both OUT1's to motor coil 1 and both OUT2's to motor coil 2. The LED's are wired to Arduino pins 6 and GND. Make sure polarity is correct. See the diagram below for more information.

Troubleshooting

The majority of my troubleshooting was trying to make the motor smooth and quiet. If the stepper motor is jittery or too loud, try adjusting the step delay and checking power connections. I will say this was the best place to use an LLM (Large Language Model). Finding the correct step sequence can be a bit tricky if not time consuming. The LLM made this much quicker. The rest of the troubleshooting involved the NTP server. For me, Serial.print("printXYZ"); was the most helpful tool. It enabled me to see what code was being performedand when.
